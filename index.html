Reported Questions

When you report what someone asked, the structure changes. Two rules govern everything: word order and tense.

01 — The Core Problem

Why questions change when reported

When you ask a question directly, English uses inverted word order and auxiliary verbs: "Where do you live?" When you report that question, both of those things disappear. The reported version looks and sounds like a statement, not a question.

Two things always change. Sometimes a third does too.

Rule 1 — Word order
No inversion. No auxiliary verb. The word order becomes subject + verb, like in a statement.
"Where does she live?"
→ He asked where she lived
(not: where did she live)
Rule 2 — Tense shift
The verb moves one step back in time. Present becomes past; past becomes past perfect.
"Are you ready?"
→ She asked if I was ready
(not: if I am ready)
Rule 3 — Yes/No connector
Yes/no questions need if or whether to link the reported question to the verb.
"Have you met him?"
→ She asked if I had met him
→ She asked whether I had met him
No question mark
A reported question is a statement. It ends with a full stop, not a question mark.
✗ She asked where I was going?
✓ She asked where I was going.
02 — Tense Reference

How tenses shift

The verb in the original question shifts one step into the past when reported. Here are all the shifts you need:

Direct question uses Reported question uses Example
present simple
is / are / do / does		 → past simple
was / were / did		 "Are you ready?" → she asked if I was ready
present continuous
is/are + -ing		 → past continuous
was/were + -ing		 "What are you doing?" → she asked what I was doing
past simple
did / went / said		 → past perfect
had + past participle		 "Where did you go?" → she asked where I had gone
present perfect
have/has + pp		 → past perfect
had + past participle		 "Have you eaten?" → she asked if I had eaten
will → would "Will you help?" → she asked if I would help
can → could "Can you swim?" → she asked if I could swim
Note Could, would, should, might, and ought to do not change — they are already past in form.
03 — Two Types

wh-questions vs yes/no questions

The two types of direct question are reported differently. The difference is in how you connect the reported clause.

wh-questions — use the wh-word
Direct "Where do you live?"
Reported He asked where I lived.
Direct "Why did she leave?"
Reported He asked why she had left.
Direct "What time will you arrive?"
Reported He asked what time I would arrive.
yes/no questions — use if or whether
Direct "Are you coming?"
Reported She asked if I was coming.
Direct "Have you seen him?"
Reported She asked whether I had seen him.
Direct "Did you eat breakfast?"
Reported She asked if I had eaten breakfast.
if vs whether Both are correct for yes/no questions. Whether is slightly more formal and is often preferred in writing.

Quick Reference
— Reported questions use statement word order — no inversion, no auxiliary: she asked where I lived
— The verb shifts one tense back: present → past, past → past perfect, will → would, can → could
— Yes/no questions need if or whether: she asked if I had eaten
— Reported questions end with a full stop, not a question mark
